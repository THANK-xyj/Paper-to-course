# Paper Course Content Philosophy

## Start With Stakes

Open with why the paper exists. The learner should understand the problem before seeing architecture, equations, or experiments.

## Teach the Claim, Not Just the Topic

Every paper course should make a distinction between:
- Topic: what the paper is about
- Claim: what the paper argues is true
- Evidence: what the paper shows
- Boundary: where the claim may stop applying

## Keep It Visual

Convert dense text into:
- Claim/evidence cards
- Method flow animations
- Figure walkthroughs
- Equation translations
- Variable cards
- Experiment tables rewritten as decision questions

## Glossary Aggressively

Tooltip field terms, acronyms, metrics, model names, and statistical language. Define terms in a way that helps the learner use them in future prompts or discussions.

## Quizzes Test Transfer

Do not ask "what does X stand for?" Ask the learner to use the paper:
- Choose the right ablation
- Predict a failure mode
- Identify which claim a result supports
- Decide whether the method fits a new use case

## Honesty Rules

- Separate paper claims from your interpretation.
- Mark inferences clearly.
- Do not oversell results.
- Mention missing details that affect reproduction.
- If figures or equations cannot be extracted, say what was unavailable and teach from the available text.
